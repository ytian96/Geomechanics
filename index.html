Interactive Mohr Circle Tool for Fracture Activation in Geothermal Reservoirs

This interactive teaching tool helps students evaluate possible fracture activation modes using in-situ stress, preexisting fracture orientation, fluid pressure, friction angle, and cohesion.

The goal is to connect the Mohr-circle representation with the physical interpretation of opening activation and shear activation in geothermal systems.

Learning objectives

  1. Explain how fracture orientation controls the normal traction, $\sigma_n$, and shear traction, $\tau$.
  2. Use the Mohr circle to visualize the traction state, $(\sigma_n,\tau)$, on a preexisting fracture.
  3. Explain how fluid pressure, $p$, reduces effective normal traction, $\sigma_n'$, and shifts the Mohr circle left.
  4. Use the Coulomb failure line, $\tau = \tan(\phi)\sigma_n' + c$, to evaluate shear activation.
  5. Distinguish among no activation, opening activation, shear activation, and combined activation.

Sign convention and criteria

This tool uses a compression-positive sign convention:

$$\sigma_1 > \sigma_3.$$

Fluid pressure reduces effective normal traction:

$$\sigma_n' = \sigma_n - p.$$

The Coulomb failure criterion for shear activation is:

$$\tau \geq \tan(\phi)(\sigma_n' + T_0) + c.$$

The opening criterion is:

$$p > \sigma_3 + T_0.$$

Assumptions

This tool uses a 2D simplification that only considers the maximum and minimum principal stresses, $\sigma_1$ and $\sigma_3$, and ignores the intermediate principal stress, $\sigma_2$.

In a real 3D stress state, the full 3D Mohr-circle representation should be used, including the circles associated with $\sigma_1$, $\sigma_2$, and $\sigma_3$.

The tension cut-off line used here is also a simplified assumption for opening-mode failure. A more realistic brittle-rock failure model can use the Griffith-Coulomb criterion, often called the Modified Griffith criterion; see, for example, Hoek and Brown's discussion of Griffith-based brittle-rock failure .

How to interpret the figures

Figure 1: Fracture orientation in physical space

The fracture orientation, $\theta$, is measured counterclockwise from the $\sigma_3$ direction to the fracture plane.

Figure 2: Traction state on the Mohr circle

The normal and shear tractions are computed as:

$$ \sigma_n = \frac{\sigma_1+\sigma_3}{2} + \frac{\sigma_1-\sigma_3}{2}\cos(2\theta), $$

$$ \tau = \frac{\sigma_1-\sigma_3}{2}\sin(2\theta). $$

A rotation by $\theta$ in physical space corresponds to a rotation by $2\theta$ on the Mohr circle.

Figure 3: Fluid pressure and activation mode

Fluid pressure shifts the Mohr circle and the fracture traction state to the left by $p$:

$$ \sigma_1' = \sigma_1 - p,\qquad \sigma_3' = \sigma_3 - p,\qquad \sigma_n' = \sigma_n - p. $$

Opening activation is identified when $p > \sigma_3 + T_0$. Shear activation on a preexisting fracture is identified when $\tau \geq \tan(\phi)\sigma_n' + c$.

Suggested exercises

Exercise 1: Effect of fracture orientation. Keep $\sigma_1$ and $\sigma_3$ fixed. Move $\theta$ from $0^\circ$ to $90^\circ$. At what orientation is $\tau$ largest?
Exercise 2: Effect of fluid pressure. Keep $\theta$ fixed and increase $p$. What happens to $\sigma_n'$? Does $\tau$ change?
Exercise 3: Opening versus shear activation. Find one parameter set where only opening is activated, one where only shear is activated, and one where both are activated.
Exercise 4: Effect of rock friction angle. Increase $\phi$. How does the Coulomb failure line change? Does shear activation become easier or harder?
Exercise 5: Effect of rock cohesion and rock tensile strength. Increase $c$ or $T_0$. How do the Coulomb failure line, tension cut-off line, and activation status change?
